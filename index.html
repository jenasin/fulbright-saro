Abstract

A peer-reviewed survey of the 2011–2026 literature shows that an engineering-grade digital twin of an individual dairy cow does not yet exist in commercial practice. What exists are well-developed building blocks — sensors, cow-level predictive models, herd-level simulators, and decision-support workflows — that have remained largely disconnected. This project proposes to spend four months at a U.S. host institution building and validating a minimum-viable, hybrid digital twin for dairy cows that runs on data farms already export every day: parlour and AMS logs, milk recording, management events, activity–rumination collars and weather.

The twin does not attempt to replace the farmer. It produces a daily, ranked, explainable worklist of cows to inspect, with reason codes a farmer can act on. The deliverables are a co-authored peer-reviewed paper, an open prototype, a reproducible benchmark over public datasets, and a deployment-ready core that the host institution and Czech partners can build on after the visit ends.

The gap this project closes

The literature is strong in monitoring and only moderately strong in closed-loop decision-making. No published system fuses these layers into a single workflow that

  • runs on the data farms already export,
  • prioritises cows under realistic operator constraints,
  • explains the prioritisation in a single sentence a farmer can verify, and
  • has been validated against alert burden, lead time and precision‑at‑top‑k, not AUROC alone.

Closing exactly this gap is what makes a four-month, single-host visit feasible and publishable.

Research questions

  1. What is the minimum data envelope — among milk / parlour / AMS logs, management records, activity–rumination collars and weather — that yields a useful cow-level twin?
  2. Does modelling an explicit cow state outperform stand-alone one-shot predictors when the goal is prioritisation, not raw classification?
  3. Which explanation format produces actionable, trust-calibrated decisions for a real farmer?

Methodology in one page

Cow state representation

Each cow is represented by a latent state with four components: a production state (lactation trajectory residuals), a perturbation state (acute deviations in yield, conductivity, flow, rumination), a fertility state (oestrus / non-oestrus / pregnant / questionable), and a risk trajectory (rolling probability of mastitis, lameness or metabolic disorder). The state is updated daily; the twin emits both a point score and an uncertainty band.

Explainability layer

  • SHAP over tree-based models for local and global attribution.
  • Reason codes against the cow’s own baseline, not the herd: “rumination −18 % vs. 7-day mean”, “rear-quarter milk flow atypically low”, “two extra refusals”.
  • Counterfactual sentences: “if rumination were within band and milking interval shorter, this cow would drop out of today’s top 20.”
  • Uncertainty banding: every score is rendered with a “screen now / watch / no action” category to protect against alert fatigue.

What the stay leaves behind — on both sides

The stay is designed so that both the host institution and the Czech research base have something durable to build on afterwards.

  • A peer-reviewed publication on the design and validation of the twin, with co-authorship for members of the host group secured from the outset. This anchors a long-term research relationship between the host institution and CULS / ÚTIA.
  • An open prototype hardened to a level at which a company or start-up — in the United States, in the Czech Republic, or jointly — could adopt it as the basis of a deployment, in the spirit of a grant whose output is a usable technology rather than a closed report.
  • A reproducible public benchmark over open dairy datasets (Zenodo, Agroscope, INRAE, Teagasc, data.gov.ie) that other groups can build on.

Together they translate a four-month visit into a multi-year contribution to Czech–U.S. scientific cooperation and to the modernisation of Central European dairy agriculture.
